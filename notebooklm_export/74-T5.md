Great question — and the honest answer is that T5+ for this specific competition is more accessible than it sounds, because the simulation environment is deterministic and lightweight compared to typical RL benchmarks.
The T5 Core: Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) MCTS doesn't require a GPU or cloud cluster — it requires a fast, accurate game simulator running in your local process within the 1-second turn budget. The architecture has four components:
The Forward Simulator A pure Python/NumPy reimplementation of the Orbit Wars turn order — comet expiration, fleet launch, production, fleet movement, planet rotation, combat resolution. This is the hardest engineering task in the whole project. It must be exactly correct (especially the continuous collision detection against the sun and moving planets) and fast enough to run ~200–500 simulated turns per second. NumPy vectorization over planet/fleet arrays is the key — avoid Python loops over individual game objects.

The Rollout Policy When you reach a leaf node, you simulate the game to completion using a fast "playout policy" — typically your T4 heuristic bot playing both sides. The quality of this rollout policy has a huge impact on MCTS signal quality. A random rollout policy converges slowly; a heuristic rollout policy converges much faster.
The Evaluation / Backpropagation At game end (or a depth limit), score the state — ship count differential is the natural metric. Backpropagate the reward up the tree.
The T6 Layer: Self-Play + Learned Evaluation T6 replaces or augments the handcrafted rollout/evaluation with a learned function, typically through one of two paths:

This gives you a local ladder — you don't need to spend submission quota to iterate. The 5 submissions/day limit means local testing is essential, not optional.
The Compute Bottleneck Is Actually Time, Not Hardware The real constraint at T5+ is the 1-second actTimeout per turn. In 500 turns, your MCTS agent has a total of 500 seconds of wall-clock compute across the whole game. With a fast NumPy simulator doing ~1,000 simple state transitions per second, you can realistically explore trees of depth 10–20 with ~100 rollouts per turn — which is enough to be meaningfully better than pure heuristics.
If you want to push toward true T6 with a learned value function but don't want to invest in GPU training infrastructure, the CMA-ES path on CPU is the most time-efficient route to a competitive bot within your 2-month window. You'd be looking at roughly:
Week 1–2: Build the correct simulator + T3/T4 heuristic baseline
Week 3–4: Wrap MCTS around it, validate locally
Week 5–6: Run CMA-ES self-play to tune evaluation weights
Week 7–8: Iterate on edge cases (comet timing, sun avoidance, 4-player FFA dynamics) and submit daily
That's a realistic T5/low-T6 trajectory from a standing start, with no GPU and no prior Kaggle competition experience.
